Why John?

John is the first read, but not the first book in the Bible.

The Bible is ordered canonically

The Bible is not arranged as a straight timeline. It is organized by genre and by the way the church received the books: Law, history, poetry, prophecy, Gospels, Acts, letters, and Revelation. That is how John can be a first read even though it is not the Bible’s first book.

John is first in the learning path

John gives a clear, direct introduction to Jesus: who he is, what he claims, why he came, and what belief in him means. The early church preserved it as one of the four Gospels, and readers often start there because it is a strong doorway into the whole Bible.

Translations

Why compare Bible translations?

What comparison helps with

  • Seeing where translators choose readability versus literal wording.
  • Spotting key terms like grace, faith, righteousness, repentance, and kingdom.
  • Understanding whether a passage is poetic, narrative, legal, or argumentative.

Simple rule of thumb

  • Begin with a readable translation.
  • Check a more literal translation when a verse feels unclear.
  • Use an interlinear or Greek tool when you want to go all the way down.

Readable

NIV, NLT, CSB. Good for first-time readers, audio listening, and momentum.

Middle ground

ESV, NASB, NKJV. Good when you want a balance between clarity and precision.

Traditions

Different kinds of Christianity, in broad families

Catholic

Emphasizes the Bible, tradition, sacraments, the historic church, and the teaching office of the church.

Orthodox

Emphasizes ancient liturgy, the early councils, the sacraments, and a strongly historical worship life.

Protestant

Emphasizes Scripture as the final authority, salvation by grace through faith, and the reform movements that came after the Reformation.

Within Protestantism

You will also hear about evangelical, mainline, Pentecostal, charismatic, Baptist, Reformed, Anglican, and non-denominational churches. These are different expressions inside the wider Protestant family.

What to compare

When you compare traditions, look at authority, sacraments, worship style, church structure, and how each group explains salvation and Christian life.

This is meant to be broad, beginner-friendly, and honest.
